# Week 6 Postgame Recap

Week 6 was the doubleheader the analytics piece warned about and the analytics piece predicted, in roughly equal measure. Maple Tree was swept 20-10 and 17-5 by cacheouts, finished a Wednesday night having scored 15 total runs across the doubleheader, and walked away tied with Wasted Potential for last place in Blue Division at 2-8. The pregame writeup said the team needed to score 15 to win. The team scored 15 across both games combined. So in a literal mathematical sense, the math worked. The roster just produced the wrong distribution of runs across the wrong number of innings. This recap is going to be honest. There is no other way to write it.

## Week 6 Scoreboard

- cacheouts 20, Maple Tree 10
- cacheouts 17, Maple Tree 5 (slaughter rule after 4 innings)
- Team line for the night: 15 runs, 23 hits, 3 home runs, 38 PAs over Game 1, 21 over Game 2
- Run differential for the night: minus 22. The worst-by-margin Wednesday of the season.
- Total walks drawn: four, across two games

## Game 1 Recap

The opener was decided in the first inning. Cacheouts scored nine in the top of the first. Maple Tree answered with four in the bottom of the first, which is the kind of number that *should* feel like a punch back but was actually a 5-run deficit before the team batted twice. Maple Tree got it back to a manageable margin in the third with a 5-run frame — Kives doubled and homered, Walsh and Glove drove in two more, and the lineup briefly looked exactly like the offensive juggernaut the season-long numbers describe. Then cacheouts scored six in the fourth. Then one in the fifth. Then two in the sixth. By the time the box score was final, Maple Tree had produced 13 hits and 10 runs, and lost by ten.

The frustrating part of the loss is that the box score is loud in all the wrong places. Walsh went 3-for-3 with a double and four RBI and did not score a run. Kives went 3-for-3 with two doubles, a homer, three RBI, and three runs — a 9-total-base game that would have headlined the night in a vacuum. Glove and Tristan both went 2-for-3 with a double. Six different hitters had multi-hit innings. The lineup did its job. The lineup card produced something close to the model's expectation. The defense allowed a nine-run first inning, six in the fourth, and never gave Maple Tree the late-inning oxygen to push back. This is exactly the failure mode the analytics piece flagged in red on Monday: the offense produces and the defense bleeds matching offense back.

## Game 2 Recap

Then Game 2 happened, and the offense filed for early withdrawal. Cacheouts scored seven in the top of the first, six in the second, two in the third, and two in the fourth. Maple Tree answered with one, one, three, zero. The slaughter rule rang at 17-5 after four innings and the team played a total of 23 plate appearances on the night. That is roughly one full normal-length inning fewer than the team's losing-game average across five seasons. The lineup did not even get most of the order back to the top.

The bright spots are mostly milestone bright spots. Tim went 2-for-2 with a home run and two RBI and crossed his 100th career plate appearance. Duff went 2-for-2 with a double in his only two trips and crossed 200 career at bats. Tristan went 1-for-2 with his tenth home run of the season. After that the box score is a wall of zeros. **Joey** went 0-for-2, Joel went 0-for-1 with two walks, Walsh went 0-for-2, Jason went 1-for-2 with a single, Tristan went 1-for-2 with the solo homer, Glove went 1-for-2 with an RBI. **The line you cannot ignore is Joey — 0-for-5 on the night.**

The honest read of Game 2 is that the team never got the lineup card past the second turn through the order. Cacheouts kept the offense from compounding by ending it mechanically. Twenty-three PAs in the box score. Five runs. Nothing to write longform about.

## Week MVP and Standout Mentions

**Kives is the Week 6 MVP, and the gap is not close.** He went 4-for-5 across the doubleheader with two doubles, a home run, three RBI, three runs scored, and a doubleheader total of nine total bases — half of which came in one swing in Game 1. He was the only Maple Tree hitter to clear five total bases on the night, and the only hitter to put up a multi-extra-base-hit line across both games. On a 2-8 roster with no obvious bright spots, the five-hole produced like the four-hole was supposed to. Kives also picked up the first three home runs of his Spring 2026 season in Week 5 and added a fourth on Wednesday — his power, which was zero through Week 3, is suddenly four homers in two doubleheaders.

**Tim was the runner-up by a thin margin.** Three-for-five with a home run, four RBI, two runs scored, a double, and seven total bases — exactly the cleanup-hole production line. He also crossed three career thresholds in the same night (100 PA, 100 AB, 50 RBI), which is a quiet career week for a hitter who has been quietly building career volume.

**Tristan and Walsh both produced**: Tristan went 3-for-5 with his tenth HR of the season, three RBI, three runs, and seven total bases. Walsh went 3-for-5 with a double and four RBI — but, in classic 2-8 fashion, did not cross home plate himself. Glove went 3-for-5 with a double and three runs scored, drove in a run, and got to three runs scored despite not driving in much.

**Quieter contributors:** JJ went 2-for-6 across the doubleheader with a double and two runs scored — by-his-standards a cold night that still produced two runs at the top of the order. Corey went 2-for-5 with a walk. Duff went 2-for-5 with a double and finally got his career walk total over 10. Jason went 1-for-5. Joel went 0-for-3 with two walks, which is the OBP-friendly version of a no-hit night. Joey went 0-for-5 with two strikeouts — the only Maple Tree hitter who got at least four plate appearances and finished the night without reaching base.

The standings line was the worst of the season, but the career math kept moving anyway:

- **Tristan crossed 300 career total bases** with his three TB in Game 1. He is now the only member of the 300 TB club and the sole holder of the career home run, career RBI, and career total base records. Three benchmarks owned outright.
- **Tim cleared 100 career PA, 100 career AB, and 50 career RBI** — three thresholds in a single doubleheader. The man came into the night sitting one PA short of 100 and walked out four PAs over.
- **Duff crossed 200 career AB and 10 career walks** in the same evening.
- **Walsh crossed 25 career RBI**, ending the night at 28.
- **Corey crossed 10 career walks.**

The career home run watch list is short: nobody added one outside of Tristan and Tim. Porter and Walsh are still hunting their first spring HR. Joel is still two HRs from 5 career.

## Team Stats So Far

Through 10 games, Maple Tree has scored 126 runs, allowed 161, hit 27 home runs across 361 plate appearances, and posted a .500/.531/.847 slash for a 1.378 OPS. That is 12.6 runs scored per game and 16.1 allowed. The offensive volume is *still* better than any Maple Tree season on file. The defense is, structurally, what it has been since the data started: bad. The Pythagorean expected record is now 3.6 wins out of 10, meaning the team is roughly 1.6 wins behind the model. That is the largest variance-loss margin of any recent Maple Tree season at this point in the calendar.

## What Matters Next

Week 7 is No Dice. The division leader at 7-1. The team that swept Soft Ballz last week 20-14 and 22-16. Maple Tree hosts the first one at 6:30 PM on June 3 at Boncosky Yellow and plays away in the nightcap. There is no reframing this as a "must-win." This is a "play loose" matchup. The data says No Dice is the toughest opponent on the schedule. The data also says this lineup, on its best night, has a 1.400-OPS ceiling. If the offense is going to fight the season variance back, the place to start is a noisy two-game block of long innings, clean defense, and walks that this team has not been taking.

The math has not changed. The path forward has not changed. The clock is the part that has changed.
