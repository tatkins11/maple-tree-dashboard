# Week 4 Postgame Recap

Week 4 was Maple Tree's first chance to find out whether the bye week was rest or rust. The answer was a vigorous "yes, both." The club went 0-2 against Wasted Talent and now sits at 1-5 in the Blue Division, but the two losses arrived as completely different species. Game 1 was a 17-16 squeaker where Maple Tree scored 16 runs and somehow still left the field shaking its head, the kind of result that needs no explanation, just a label that reads "exhibit A." Game 2 was a 16-4 blowout that ended on the slaughter rule and required no exhibit at all, because nothing about it required preservation. Add the two together and a single opponent outscored Maple Tree 33-20 on the night, which is hard to do unless one of the two games gets cleanly stuck in the lobby.

## Week 4 Scoreboard

- Wasted Talent 17, Maple Tree 16
- Wasted Talent 16, Maple Tree 4
- Team line for the week: 20 runs, 28 hits, 3 home runs, 47 total bases across 65 plate appearances, good for roughly a .459/.492/.770 slash with a 1.262 OPS — which is still loud, but loud in only one of the two games
- Run differential for the night: minus 13. Bullseyes night was minus 9. The bye week did not appear to redistribute innings.

## Game 1 Recap

The opener was the kind of softball game that quietly drives managers toward new hobbies. Maple Tree scored 16 runs on 21 hits, including home runs from Glove, Tristan, and Tim, and lost by one. Three different hitters went 3-for-4 — Glove (1B, 2B, HR, 4 RBI, 2 runs, 7 total bases), Tristan (1B, 2B, HR, 3 RBI, 2 runs, 7 total bases), and Jj (1B, 1B, 2B, a walk, three runs scored) — and another three multi-hit games filled in behind them. That is not a slow night at the plate. That is a complete turn through the lineup with everybody contributing, and the team still came out on the wrong side of the scoreboard.

The issue was, predictably, the other half-inning. Wasted Talent scored 17 of their own, which is the kind of number that takes a slugfest from "we should win this" to "we cannot afford a single quiet frame." Maple Tree did not have a quiet frame, but slowpitch arithmetic is unforgiving: when the other team beats you by one in a 33-run game, the answer is almost always a single defensive inning that became a town hall.

The encouraging part is that the offense did exactly what an offense is supposed to do in a game where the pitcher is essentially wearing both team's jerseys: it produced. All eleven Maple Tree hitters reached safely at least once, nobody had an empty trip without a walk, and only Corey escaped the night without either a run scored or an RBI to his name — and even Corey still had a hit. That is, structurally, a near-perfect offensive box score. It just lost.

## Game 2 Recap

Then Game 2 happened, and the offense filed for short notice. Wasted Talent scored 16 runs and triggered the slaughter rule around the time the lineup card finished its second cup of coffee. Every Maple Tree hitter got exactly two plate appearances — the lineup did not even get a full third turn through — and the final line was 4 runs, 7 hits, no home runs, and a team slash that read like the offensive identity from Game 1 had been deported.

Slomka had two of the seven hits and went 2-for-2. Tim drove in one with a double and added another double in his two trips, which is roughly two-thirds of all the extra-base damage Maple Tree mustered. Glove tripled and walked. Jason added an RBI single in his only at bat of the night. After that the box score is a wall of zeros, including hitless trips for Tristan, Kives, Jj, Joey, Duff, and Corey. There is no game-flow story to tell about a game that did not flow.

The honest read is that Game 2 became unwinnable mechanically before the offense could fix anything, and once the slaughter rule starts ticking the second-game lineup card stops getting full turns. Even with that caveat, a four-run total on two PA apiece is a slow night for this group, and it's the first game of the season where the offense was unambiguously the problem.

## Week MVP and Standout Mentions

Glove takes the Week 4 MVP nod, and it is not particularly close. Across two games and six PA he went 4-for-5 with a home run, a triple, a double, a walk, five RBI, three runs, and 10 total bases. The doubleheader looked like one full game's worth of production from one hitter — and one of those at bats came in the four-run blowout where almost nobody else made noise. The other load-bearing beam under the MVP case is that Glove crossed the 100 career RBI threshold during the week, ending Week 4 at 104. Until this Wednesday the 100 RBI club had exactly one member.

Tim is the runner-up. He went 4-for-6 across the two nights with a home run, three doubles, three RBI, three runs scored, and the only two extra-base hits Maple Tree managed in Game 2. He also pushed his own career numbers across two thresholds in the same week: he cleared 50 career hits and ran his career doubles total into double digits. For a hitter still building career volume, putting a 25-games-played milestone, a hits milestone, and a doubles milestone on the same week's invoice is good business even on a losing team.

Other standouts deserve their flowers too. Tristan's Game 1 line was 3-for-4 with a homer and three RBI, even though the nightcap held him to 0-for-2. Jj reached four times and scored four runs across the two games. Walsh kept producing in his quiet way — 3-for-6 with a double, two runs, two RBI — and Kives added three hits including a triple. Slomka quietly went 3-for-6 and would probably have a louder week if anyone had remembered to schedule the second offense.

## Team Stats So Far

Through six completed games and one bye, Maple Tree has scored 74 runs, allowed 107, hit 15 home runs across 220 team plate appearances, and posted a .480/.527/.790 slash for a 1.317 OPS. That works out to about 12.3 runs scored per game and 17.8 runs allowed per game — the offense is performing at a top-of-the-division level and the run prevention is performing at a "let's not look directly at it" level. The structural story of the season has not changed since Week 2. It has just added one more data point with extra emphasis.

Tristan still leads the team in basically every rate stat: 2.548 OPS, .667 AVG, seven home runs and 17 RBI in 22 plate appearances. Glove is right behind on volume — 14 hits, 15 RBI — and Tim and Joel round out the four-deep top tier. Below the headline names the depth keeps producing, with eight of the regular hitters carrying a 1.000+ OPS through six games. That is not a lineup with an offensive problem. That is a lineup with a different problem dressed as an offensive problem.

## What Matters Next

Week 5 is Wasted Potential, who sit at 1-7 in the current snapshot and are the one team in the division currently behind Maple Tree. This is the schedule's officially-stamped "if you are going to right the season, the next two weeks are when you do it" moment. Game 1 against Wasted Talent already proved that a good Maple Tree offensive night still scores 16 runs; the actual assignment now is to put two full games together in the same evening instead of one big offense plus one empty offense.

The mission is straightforward: keep producing the way the season slash line says this group can produce, then play one tidy inning of run prevention per game, and watch the win column finally pretend to start working. Anything other than a positive standings result against Wasted Potential next week would be a quiet upset against the bare math of the matchup.

In the meantime: nobody panic, the offense is fine, and Glove has 100 RBI. That feels like a reasonable place to leave it.
